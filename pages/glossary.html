Glossary

Allowance Race

A type of race where the weight a horse carries is determined by the money it has previously won or the number of races it has won. It is a step above maiden races but below stakes races.

Apprentice Jockey

A jockey who is in the early stages of their career and is under a license that allows them to claim a weight allowance to compensate for their lack of experience.

Backstretch

The straight part of the racecourse opposite the homestretch, or the area of the track containing the stables and training facilities.

Blinkers

A piece of equipment, a hood with cups attached, placed on a horse's head to limit its peripheral vision. This helps the horse focus on what is in front of it and can improve concentration.

Breeders' Cup

A prestigious year-end championship series of Grade I Thoroughbred horse races in the United States, which often attracts top international competitors, including from Japan.

Classic Race

A race of major historic importance. In many countries, this refers to the traditional series of races for three-year-olds. In Japan, this specifically refers to the five premier races for three-year-olds.

Dirt Racing

Racing on a surface composed of soil, sand, and clay. While turf is primary in Europe, dirt racing is very prominent in Japan and North America.

Furlong

A unit of distance equal to one-eighth of a mile, or 220 yards (approximately 201 meters). Race distances are commonly measured in furlongs.

G1, G2, G3 (Grade 1, 2, 3)

The highest level of race classification, indicating the prestige, purse, and quality of the competition. G1 is the pinnacle, followed by G2 and G3. The Japanese racing calendar is built around its G1 events.

Handicap

A race where the racing secretary assigns weights to each horse in an attempt to equalize their chances of winning. Theoretically, after weights are assigned, every horse should finish in a dead heat.

Homestretch (or Stretch)

The final straight section of the racetrack from the final turn to the finish line.

Jockey

The rider of a horse in a race. In Japan, jockeys must graduate from the rigorous JRA Racing School.

Japan Racing Association (JRA)

The governing body that operates most of the major racecourses in Japan (e.g., Tokyo, Kyoto, Hanshin). It is responsible for the highest level of Thoroughbred racing in the country, including all Grade 1 races.

JPN1, JPN2, JPN3

The Japanese grading system for races that are not internationally classified but are of national importance. These are primarily stakes races that sit just below the international G1, G2, G3 level.

Local Racing (NAR)

Racing governed by the National Association of Racing (NAR), which operates at smaller, regional tracks across Japan. It is separate from the JRA, with its own jockeys, horses, and race calendar.

Maiden

A horse that has never won a race. Maiden races are exclusively for such horses.

Nakayama Racecourse

A major JRA racecourse located in Funabashi, Chiba. It is known for its figure-eight layout and hosts prestigious races like the Arima Kinen (The Grand Prix) and the Nakayama Grand Jump.

Oikiri

A Japanese term describing a horse that is a strong, front-runner, often setting a fast pace from the start.

Paddock

The enclosure where horses are saddled and paraded before the race, allowing bettors and officials to inspect their condition.

Post Position

The stall number a horse starts from in the starting gate. A low or high post position can be an advantage or disadvantage depending on the track's layout.

Silks (or Colors)

The colorful jacket and cap worn by a jockey, which represent the owner of the horse. Each set of silks is unique and registered.

Sodashi

A famous white Thoroughbred filly who won multiple G1 races in Japan, including the 2021 Oka Sho (Japanese 1000 Guineas). Her name and color made her a national sensation.

Stakes Race (Stakes)

A high-level race where owners must pay a fee to enter their horse, with the fees contributing to the total prize money (purse). The most prestigious stakes races are Graded Stakes.

Stayer

A horse that is specialized in running long-distance races.

Steeplechase

A type of horse race in which jockeys ride horses over obstacles, including fences and water jumps. The Nakayama Grand Jump is a famous G1 steeplechase in Japan.

Takarakuji

The Japanese lottery, a portion of whose revenue is used to fund the JRA, making it a unique publicly-funded model for horse racing.

Tokyo Racecourse

The flagship racecourse of the JRA, located in Fuchu, Tokyo. It is one of the world's largest and most modern racecourses, hosting the Japan Cup.

Triple Crown

A series of three classic races for three-year-olds. The Japanese Triple Crown consists of the Satsuki Sho (2000 Guineas), Tokyo Yushun (Japanese Derby), and Kikuka Sho (Japanese St. Leger). There is a separate fillies' Triple Crown.

Turf Racing

Racing on a grass surface. This is the most common and traditional surface for elite racing in Europe, Australia, and Japan.

Umatoku

A Japanese slang term combining "uma" (horse) and "otaku" (geek/enthusiast), referring to a dedicated horse racing fan who deeply studies bloodlines, past performances, and statistics.

Walkover

A race where only one horse is officially entered, allowing it to canter to the finish line to claim the prize money. Extremely rare in modern racing.

Win-5 (Wide Five)

A popular and challenging bet type unique to Japanese racing where a bettor must pick the winners of five designated races on a single race card. It features a massive, rolling prize pool.

Yushun

A Japanese word meaning "excellence" or "supreme victory," often used in the names of major races, most notably the Tokyo Yushun (Japanese Derby).

Japan Cup

The premier international invitational turf race in Japan, held at Tokyo Racecourse. It attracts top horses from around the world and is one of the richest races on the global calendar.

Arima Kinen (The Grand Prix)

A famous G1 race held at Nakayama Racecourse. Unique in the world, the field is partially determined by a national fan vote, making it "the race of the people" and often the highest-bet race in the world.

Tenno Sho (Emperor's Cup)

There are two G1 Tenno Sho races held each year: the Spring Tenno Sho at Kyoto Racecourse (3200m) and the Autumn Tenno Sho at Tokyo Racecourse (2000m). They are among the most prestigious races in the Japanese calendar.

Oka Sho (Japanese 1000 Guineas)

The first leg of the Japanese Fillies' Triple Crown, a G1 race for three-year-old fillies run over 1600 meters at Hanshin Racecourse.

Satsuki Sho (Japanese 2000 Guineas)

The first leg of the Japanese Triple Crown, a G1 race for three-year-old colts and fillies run over 2000 meters at Nakayama Racecourse.

Tokyo Yushun (Japanese Derby)

The second leg and most prestigious race of the Japanese Triple Crown, a G1 race for three-year-olds run over 2400 meters at Tokyo Racecourse.
